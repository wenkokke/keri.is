The Disaster is an improv form inspired by the tabletop game [Fiasco]. It uses the structure of the [Harold] and [La Ronde], rigged to explode in a disastrous finale.

![The Scene Structure for The Disaster](./the-disaster.dot?roughness=0.75)

## Suggestion

The suggestion should either _be_ or _inspire_ a genre.

Let's keep things simple for our example, and say our suggestion is "small town drama".

## Confessions

The Confessions are short monologues that introduces the characters and the relationships between them. The players take turns to step out on stage and deliver a short monologue that introduces who their character is and what their character _needs_. You edit the previous confession by stepping out and starting your own.

The order in which the players deliver their confessions determines the order in which they'll play the 1st and 2nd Beats. Let's say we have four players, **Sam**, **Cat**, **Mac**, and **Tay**, who deliver their confessions in that order:

- Confession A. **Sam**
- Confession B. **Cat**
- Confession C. **Mac**
- Confession D. **Tay**

That means the 1st and 2nd Beats will be:

- Scene A/B. **Sam** and **Cat**
- Scene B/C. **Cat** and **Mac**
- Scene C/D. **Mac** and **Tay**
- Scene D/A. **Tay** and **Sam**

Let's look at a little example:

- Confession A. **Sam** steps out and delivers a confession that establishes who they are and what they need:

  > Sam: I've got to score drugs for Tay's party. Her parents are away, so she's throwing a party, and somehow I got invited! I _need_ her to think I'm cool, and what's cooler than drugs?!

- Confession B. **Cat** edits **Sam**'s confession by stepping out and starting her own. She establishes who she is, what she needs, and how she could fulfil **Sam**'s need:

  > Cat: I told my husband I've been selling drugs. He didn't take it well... I've been sleeping on a cot in the shed for a week. I'm afraid he'll never forgive me. I _need_ him to understand. I've booked a trip for us for this weekend, so we can get away somewhere nice and talk...

- Confession C. **Mac** edits **Cat**'s confession by stepping out and starting his own. He establishes who he is, what he _needs_, and how he could fulfil **Cat**'s need:

  > Mac: My wife and I have been having some issues... I feel Tay blames me... We didn't tell her what was really going on—she's just too young to hear about drugs—but my relationship with my daughter is important to me... I _need_ her to see my side of things...

- Confession D. **Tay** edits **Mac**'s confession by stepping out and starting her own. She establishes who she is, what she needs, how she could fulfil **Sam**'s need:

  > Tay: I think... I'm gay? I think... I've got a crush on Sam? I told Sam I'm throwing a party this weekend. Why did I tell them that?! There's no party, it's just them. I just _need_ some space to figure this out... I just wish dad would get off my ass. He keeps trying to get me to hang out... I told mom to take him on a trip... Maybe that way he'll finally forgive her for forgetting their anniversary... Ugh, he's being such an asshole about it.

The character's _needs_ should form a closed loop. To see what that means, let's have a look at one way our example Confessions might play out:

> **Cat** tries to help **Sam** and promises to sell her the drugs. **Cat** tells **Mac** she's doing one last deal. Upset, he cancels the couples trip he'd planned for the weekend. With her parents at home, **Tay** has to cancel her house party... the very reason **Sam** wanted those drugs in the first place.

Imagine our four characters in a circle, connected by a rope. When **Cat** takes a step towards **Sam**, it pulls on **Mac**, which pulls on **Tay**, which pulls **Sam** away from **Cat**. That's what you're going for!

Each pair of characters should be connected by a _need_ that pulls on them, and those _needs_ all pull in the same _direction_.

Here's a recipe you can follow:

- If you're the first to step out, you only have to introduce your character and your character's _need_. That's it!

- If you're _not_ first, you should choose a character that is _able_ to fulfil the previous character's _need_, but shouldn't necessarily _want_ to fulfil that _need_.

  **Example**. If **Mac** says he _needs_ a fancy cake for his wife's birthday party, **Tay** could be an amateur baker or a fence with a load of hot cakes, but whatever she chooses, her character should be _able_ to get that fancy cake. If **Tay** chose to be **Mac**'s work buddy with no talent for baking, it'll be difficult to motivate the scenes: **Mac** _needs_ a cake, so why'd he come to **Tay**?

  **Example**. If **Sam** says they _need_ money to open a cupcake shop, then **Cat** could be a wealthy but stingy aunt, or the chair of a corrupt small business loans fund. However, **Cat** probably shouldn't be a billionaire with a love for cupcakes and giving away money, as it'd be difficult to find tension in the scenes: **Cat** _wants_ and is _able_ to give **Sam** the money, so why not just get it over with?

- If you're last, you should choose a _need_ that you know the first character is _able_ to fulfil. They've already gone, so it's up to you to tie the knot!

- You should choose a _need_ that the previous character cannot fulfil. Be specific, and avoid _needs_ that just about anybody can fulfil.

  **Example**. Let's say **Tay** says she _needs_ balloon animals for her party and **Sam** says she's a clown who _needs_ to get out there and meet new people. Uh-oh, we're in trouble! **Sam**'s _need_—while admittedly very relatable—isn't very specific, and could be fulfilled by just about anybody—including **Tay**. If **Tay** invites **Sam** to her party, that breaks the loop: **Sam** got what she _needs_ from **Tay**, so why would she need **Cat**? Worse, it'll be difficult to steer away from a mutually beneficial resolution between **Tay** and **Sam**, because **Tay**'s _need_ actively pulls **Sam** towards it.

Whew. That was a long one, but there's good news: we've done all the heavy lifting. The Confessions tied our characters into an inescapable loop. All we have to do now is to play with it.

## 1st Beats

The 1st beats are two person scenes. Each scene focuses on one player's need. For example, the A/B scene with **Sam** and **Cat** should focus on **Sam**'s need to be seen as cool.

:::la-ronde-scenes

- 1st Beat A/B. **Sam** and **Cat** play a scene focused on **Sam**.
- 1st Beat B/C. **Cat** and **Mac** play a scene focused on **Cat**.
- 1st Beat C/D. **Mac** and **Tay** play a scene focused on **Mac**.
- 1st Beat D/A. **Tay** and **Sam** play a scene focused on **Tay**.

:::

At some point during each scene, the backline shouts out whether or not the scene will end well for the focused player. If the scene ends well for the focused player, that _does not mean_ their need is resolved.

## The Omen

The Omen is a group game with a prophetic quality, foreshadowing the events of [The Disaster] in the style of an [Eventé].

For the players, The Omen provides an opportunity to generate new ideas and form consensus on where the story is going. There's no set structure for The Omen, but there's lots of forms that work well:

:::disc

- A group scene with new characters, set after the events of [The Disaster].
- Sound and Movement
- Scene Painting
- _etc._

:::

## 2nd Beats

The 2nd beats are two person scenes. Like the 1st Beats, each scene focuses on one player's _need_.

- 2nd Beat A/B. **Sam** and **Cat** play a scene focused on **Sam**.
- 2nd Beat B/C. **Cat** and **Mac** play a scene focused on **Cat**.
- 2nd Beat C/D. **Mac** and **Tay** play a scene focused on **Mac**.
- 2nd Beat D/A. **Tay** and **Sam** play a scene focused on **Tay**.

The stakes in the 2nd Beat should be raised, and may be informed by the pressures exerted by the previous character's need and the second character's needs.

## The Disaster

The Disaster is a Monoscene in which all accumulated tension explodes and The finale is a big group game in which all the accumulated tension explodes, and something goes horribly wrong. The team should cash in on any cheque written during The Omen.
